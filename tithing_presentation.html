TITHING AND CHRISTIAN GIVING

A Catholic Perspective

Biblical History, Church Teaching, and Modern Practice

What is Tithing?

Definition: Giving one-tenth (10%) of one's income or produce

Key Point: The concept has a rich biblical history that evolved from the Old Testament to New Testament times, and continues to inform Christian giving today.

Today we will explore:

  • The biblical origins and development of tithing
  • How the New Testament transformed giving
  • What the Catholic Church actually teaches
  • Practical application for Catholics today

Pre-Mosaic Law Period

Abraham and Melchizedek

Genesis 14:17-20 - Abraham gave one-tenth of his war spoils to Melchizedek

Who was Melchizedek?

Hebrews 7:3 describes him as a mysterious figure:

  • Without father or mother, without genealogy
  • Without beginning of days or end of life
  • A priest-king of Salem (Jerusalem)

Significance: This is the first biblical mention of tithing, showing it existed before formal law.

During the Mosaic Law

Three Types of Tithes

1First Tithe: Given to Levites and priests as their inheritance (Numbers 18:21-24)

2Second Tithe: Used for annual festival celebrations by the owner's household and Levites (Deuteronomy 14:22-27)

3Third Tithe: Provided every third year for Levites and vulnerable people (Deuteronomy 14:28-29)

Note: Biblical tithing primarily involved agricultural produce - grain, fruit, livestock. This context is important for understanding passages like Malachi 3:10.

New Testament Period

A New Approach to Giving

The early Christian church did not mandate the Old Testament tithing system. Instead, the apostles established new principles:

  • Communal Sharing - Believers shared resources freely (Acts 2:44-45, 4:32-36)
  • Regular, Proportional Giving - Set aside money regularly according to income (1 Corinthians 16:2)
  • Cheerful, Voluntary - Give freely, not under compulsion (2 Corinthians 9:7)
  • According to Ability - Gifts acceptable based on what one has (2 Corinthians 8:12)

Key Transition: From Law to Grace - from rigid obligation to generous responsibility

Catholic Church Teaching

Built on Apostolic Foundation

Ephesians 2:20 - "The Church is built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, with Christ Jesus himself as the chief cornerstone."

The Catholic Church follows the teaching and example of the apostles, balancing:

  • The duty to give (real obligation)
  • Freedom in how much (proportional to means)
  • Cheerful generosity (willing sacrifice)

The Obligation to Support the Church

Canon 222 - Code of Canon Law

"The Christian faithful are obliged to assist with the needs of the Church so that the Church has what is necessary for:

  • Divine worship
  • Works of the apostolate and charity
  • The decent support of ministers"

This is NOT optional! Catholics have a serious duty to support the Church. This obligation flows from baptism, justice, charity, and the Fifth Precept of the Church.

The Fifth Precept of the Church

"You shall help to provide for the needs of the Church"

What This Means:

  • Supporting the Church is a binding precept for all Catholics
  • It ranks alongside attending Mass on Sundays and receiving the Eucharist
  • The obligation is real and serious
  • However, the specific amount is flexible based on your circumstances

Reference: Catechism of the Catholic Church, paragraphs 2043-2046

How Much Should Catholics Give?

The Church's Balanced Approach

The Church does NOT mandate a specific percentage (like 10%)

Instead, the Church teaches:

  • Proportional to Income - Give according to your means and circumstances (CCC 2043)
  • Regular and Planned - Systematic giving, not sporadic
  • Generous and Sacrificial - Should involve real sacrifice, not just surplus
  • Multiple Forms - Money, time, talents, treasures

Many Catholics use 10% as a practical guideline, but it's not required by Church law.

Catholic Teaching Avoids Two Extremes

✗LEGALISM: "You must give exactly 10% or you're sinning"

✗MINIMALISM: "Since there's no percentage required, I'll give very little"

✓THE CATHOLIC WAY: "I have a serious obligation to support the Church generously and proportionally, according to my means, with a cheerful and grateful heart"

"God loves a cheerful giver" - 2 Corinthians 9:7

What Does the Church Need Support For?

Our contributions support:

  • Divine Worship - Maintaining churches, liturgical items, sacred music
  • Evangelization & Education - Religious education, RCIA, youth ministry
  • Works of Charity - St. Vincent de Paul, Catholic Charities, social services
  • Fair Compensation - Clergy and Church workers deserve just wages
  • Seminary Formation - Training future priests
  • Care for the Poor - Direct assistance to those in need

A Serious Warning

Exploitation of God's People

Some ministers have misapplied Malachi 3:10 by linking the "blessing" portion exclusively to personal enrichment and the "prosperity gospel."

The Problem:

  • Enriching themselves while ignoring the poor and vulnerable
  • Creating a transactional view: "give to get rich"
  • Forgetting that the Third Tithe was for the vulnerable (Deut 14:28-29)

Church Teaching: Concern for the poor is a requirement of justice and charity (CCC 2443-2449). Church leaders are stewards, not owners, of God's resources.

Personal Discernment for Catholics

Questions to Ask Yourself:

  • Am I fulfilling my obligation to support the Church?
  • Is my giving proportional to what God has given me?
  • Does my giving involve real sacrifice, or only surplus?
  • Am I giving cheerfully and gratefully?
  • Am I also supporting the poor and vulnerable?

Pray and reflect: What is God calling me to give? Remember, it's not just about money - time, talents, and treasures all matter.

Practical Recommendations

A Balanced Approach to Catholic Giving

Many spiritual directors suggest distributing support across:

  • Parish Support - Regular monthly/weekly contributions to your home parish
  • Diocesan Collections - Special collections for diocesan needs
  • Direct Charity - St. Vincent de Paul Society, Catholic Charities, food pantries
  • Missionary Work - Supporting evangelization efforts locally and globally

Remember: Whether you give 10%, more, or less, what matters is that you're fulfilling your duty with generosity, sacrifice, and joy.

Remember the Vulnerable

James 2:15-16 - "If a brother or sister is poorly clothed and lacking in daily food, and one of you says to them, 'Go in peace, be warmed and filled,' without giving them the things needed for the body, what good is that?"

Matthew 25:40 - "Truly, I say to you, as you did it to one of the least of these my brothers, you did it to me."

True biblical giving includes care for the poor. This isn't optional - it's central to our faith and Church teaching.

Conclusion: Key Takeaways

  • Catholics MUST give - It's a serious obligation (Canon 222, Fifth Precept)
  • No fixed percentage - Give proportionally according to your means
  • Real sacrifice required - Not just surplus, but genuine generosity
  • Cheerful attitude - Give joyfully, not grudgingly
  • Multiple purposes - Worship, evangelization, charity, ministers
  • Remember the poor - Direct care for the vulnerable is essential
  • 10% can be a helpful guideline - But adjust to your circumstances
